# Question Design Rules

## Rule 1

Ask for scenes, not slogans.

Prefer:

- "What happened?"
- "What kind of person came to you?"
- "What did they say?"

Avoid:

- "What is your brand?"
- "What persona do you want?"

## Rule 2

Ask for evidence before abstraction.

Get:

- examples
- exact wording
- customer type
- purchase reason

Then summarize.

## Rule 3

Use different question directions for different client types.

Zero-based:

- who do you naturally help
- who do you understand deeply
- who trusts you fastest

Validated:

- who already paid
- who closed fastest
- who referred others

## Rule 4

Every module must serve later analysis.

Later analysis includes:

- user profiles
- user lifecycle
- persona positioning

If a question does not support one of those, remove it.

Questions should also support later content targeting.

At minimum, Discovery must make it possible to answer:

- who has already bought
- who is closest to buying
- who should be filtered out
- what each of those portraits stops for, fears, misunderstands, and values

## Rule 5

Mark inference-heavy outputs.

For zero-based clients, later outputs are often hypothesis-first.  
Do not present them as proven fact.

## Rule 6

Do not stop at the client's self-description.

Always push toward downstream-customer description.

Prefer:

- "Describe the last real buyer."
- "Describe the person most likely to buy next."
- "Describe the person who often asks but should not buy."

Avoid stopping at:

- "Who is your audience?"
- "Who do you want?"
- "What industry do you serve?"

## Rule 7

For later broad-traffic and semi-vertical work, customer portraits must include more than demographics.

Try to get:

- identity
- current pressure
- real-life situation
- repeated misunderstanding
- trust trigger
- buying trigger
- disqualifying traits

## Rule 8

Capture mother-topic seeds before abstract positioning.

Do not wait until Persona to ask:

- what the client repeatedly judges
- what repeated mistakes the client keeps seeing
- what scenes the client can speak about with detail
- what stance the client naturally holds

These are the raw materials for later mother-topic selection.

Without them, later content output becomes generic.

## Rule 9

Do not ask near-close portrait questions as vague business questions.

Avoid:

- "Who is your ideal customer?"
- "Who is easier to convert?"

Prefer:

- "Who usually closes fastest?"
- "What kind of person talks with you for a while and then finally decides?"
- "What exact doubt do they still have before paying?"
- "What exact event or sentence usually pushes them over the line?"
- "Who looks close, but in reality almost never closes?"

Near-close portrait collection must produce usable user tags, not only sales commentary.
